Arabic, a language with a rich history and diverse linguistic landscape, is spoken across the Arab world in various forms known as Arabic dialects. These dialects exhibit linguistic variations that reflect the cultural, historical, and geographical diversity of the Arab nations. The term "dialects" refers to the regional variations of Arabic spoken by communities in different countries, each carrying its unique linguistic characteristics. Our project specifically focuses on three major Arabic dialects: Iraqi, Levantine, and Egyptian. These dialects represent key linguistic varieties within the Arab world, each with its own historical evolution, phonetic distinctions, and lexical peculiarities. Understanding the nuances of Iraqi, Levantine, and Egyptian dialects is pivotal for learners and researchers alike, as these dialects encapsulate the essence of daily life, cultural heritage, and linguistic diversity in their respective regions. While Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) serves as a common denominator, our project delves into the intricacies of these three major dialects, providing a comprehensive exploration of their unique features.
